Hello, world!

August 25, 2018

Noah Lange

Elevator pitch: Markwright lets you use web technologies — specifically, Markdown and SASS — to generate print content.

This isn't a new idea — PrinceXML has operated in this space for years, and WKHTMLtoPDF is perhaps the pre-eminent open source offering — but crucially, Markwright is a desktop application instead of a command-line one. It's designed for semi-technical users, ones who are competent enough to get started writing HTML and CSS, but not quite ready to delve into the land of bash, pipes and command-line arguments.

Markwright inhabits a space somewhere between a traditional DTP application and something like Prince — it's code-driven, but it's also visual. It's not interactive per se, but it's considerably more responsive than a command-line workflow.

Perhaps the best analogue is TeXShop, a *TeX editor and previewer. (If you're into TeX and working on MacOS, it's great software and I highly recommend it.) Unlike TeXShop, Markwright uses web technologies. As a result, it's considerably more accessible for semi-technical users.

Take a look. It's firmly in alpha territory, so there are a variety of missing and half-baked features, but the core product is reasonably stable for smaller projects.
